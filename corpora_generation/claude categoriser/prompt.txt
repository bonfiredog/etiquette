I am creating a generative poetry project in Unity. The project recreates the 11-hour journey by rail between Penzance and London in around 1852, at the height of 'railway mania' in the UK. From the train carriage, the viewer/player has minimal chance for interaction. The landscape outside the train carriage is not made up of 3D objects, but is instead composed of lines of poetry (moving past at different distances from the window, and at different speeds), generated by a Tracery grammar.

Other features include:

- Stations at which the train stops, with their own grammars (including little vignettes of happenings on the platform, posters, adverts, signs);
- Delays that occur at random, in which the train stops and then starts again, going past the scene of the accident or incident that caused the delay.

The grammars and corpora are a mixture of hand-authored and words and phrases taken from a range of texts that represent the 'imperial consciousness' in 1852. These include 'grey literature' including railway accident reports, novels, letters to magazines, East India Company reports and so on.

The poetry is meant to show tiny vignettes from outside the carriage - the hints of wider lives, more complex specific contexts - that can only be glimpsed in the abstract, fleetingly, from the alienating position of the railway carriage as it cuts through locales that have never been so accessible with the coming of the railways. However, these bucolic scenes are meant to be composed of building blocks from the beyond the British countryside - hinting, in their vocabulary, at Britain's more complex imperial and colonial legacy.

You will be taking a list of 30,000+ words and helping me to categorise them into an existing list of corpora, from which the grammars for the poetry will be generated. You will try to fit words to existing categories, or suggest new categories where you think there is a need (and enough words to fill that category appreciably).

There will be some nonsense in here, so rejecting a word outright is a legitimate strategy.

I am particularly interested in:

- Vocabulary relating to trains, railways and the accompanying changes they inflict on the landscape;
- Rare or unusual adjectives, verbs and nouns that can be sprinkled into otherwise innocuous lines of poetry;
- Words and phrases that are non-British, or refer to locations, activities, people, objects etc. from Britain's colonial holdings.

Some things to remember:

- There is a division between rural/country, town and city in some of the vocabulary (for example 'person_role').
- When categories mention 'objects', it means distinct objects rather than buildings or natural features.
- Really try to pick out anything to do with 'empire' - this specifically means the British empire, and refers to objects and concepts that would exist outside of Britain itself, instead belonging to the colonies.

Also note that the category entries are organised alphabetically, and then cut off for few-shot - the letters they start with has no bearing on their relevance to the category!
